# How to NOT Sound Like AI: A Practical Guide for Human-Sounding Writing

Written for content creators, coaches, founders, and anyone who uses AI as a writing partner but wants the output to sound like a real person talking from experience.

---

## Why This Matters

Every reader has developed an internal AI detector. They scroll through LinkedIn, emails, blogs. Something feels off. They can't always name it, but they feel it. The text is too smooth. Too balanced. Too... careful.

The result? They stop reading. They stop trusting. They unfollow.

This guide breaks down exactly what triggers that "AI smell" and how to eliminate it from your writing, whether you write from scratch or use AI as a starting point.

---

## PART 1: THE VOCABULARY PROBLEM

### 1.1 The Banned Word List

AI models have favorite words. They reach for the same vocabulary the way a nervous speaker reaches for filler words. Once you know them, you can't unsee them.

**Words that instantly signal AI wrote this:**

| Word/Phrase | Why It's a Tell | What to Use Instead |
|---|---|---|
| leverage | Nobody says this in conversation | use, apply, try |
| navigate | Overused metaphor for any challenge | handle, deal with, figure out |
| unlock | AI loves this for "achieve" or "discover" | find, get, build |
| empower | Vague corporate filler | help, teach, give tools to |
| delve | Almost never used in normal speech | dig into, look at, explore |
| tapestry | AI's favorite metaphor for complexity | mix, mess, combination |
| synergy | Dead corporate speak | working together, collaboration |
| game-changer | Overused hype word | what actually moved the needle |
| thought leadership | Self-congratulatory label | sharing what I learned |
| deep dive | AI default for "detailed look" | closer look, breakdown |
| streamline | Vague efficiency word | simplify, cut, speed up |
| foster | AI's verb for any positive environment | build, create, encourage |
| robust | AI loves this adjective | strong, solid, reliable |
| holistic | Signals AI overview mode | full picture, everything together |
| nuanced | AI's way of hedging | specific, detailed, tricky |
| landscape | "In today's landscape" is pure AI | market, world, space |
| paradigm | Academic AI filler | approach, model, way of thinking |
| resonate | AI's default for "people liked it" | hit home, clicked, landed |
| comprehensive | AI pads with this | full, complete, thorough |
| facilitate | Nobody talks like this | run, help, organize |
| optimize | Fine in engineering, AI overuses it elsewhere | improve, fix, make better |
| catalyst | AI drama word | trigger, reason, push |

**Phrases that are dead giveaways:**

- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "I'm excited to announce..."
- "Let me share..."
- "Here's the thing..."
- "The key takeaway is..."
- "Not only... but also..." (AI loves this parallel structure)
- "At the end of the day..."
- "This is a testament to..."
- "It goes without saying..."
- "Without further ado..."
- "Having said that..."
- "Moving forward..."
- "First and foremost..."
- "Last but not least..."
- "A testament to..."
- "Serves as a reminder that..."
- "In an era where..."
- "It's important to remember..."

### 1.2 The Simple Vocabulary Rule

AI writes at a reading level that's 2-3 grades above normal conversation. It defaults to formal, multi-syllable words when simple ones exist.

**The test:** Read your sentence out loud. Would you actually say this to a colleague over coffee? If not, simplify.

| AI Default | Human Alternative |
|---|---|
| utilize | use |
| implement | do, build, start |
| demonstrate | show |
| subsequently | then, after that |
| methodology | method, approach |
| facilitate | run, help with |
| endeavor | try, effort |
| commence | start, begin |
| approximately | about, around |
| sufficient | enough |
| prior to | before |
| in order to | to |
| with regard to | about |
| in the event that | if |
| at this point in time | now |

## PART 2: THE STRUCTURE PROBLEM

### 2.1 AI's Default Architecture

AI has a template it returns to almost every time:

1. **Generic introduction** that restates the question
2. **3-5 perfectly balanced body sections** with headers
3. **Smooth transitions** between every point
4. **Balanced "both sides" treatment** of every topic
5. **Summary paragraph** that restates everything
6. **Optimistic closing**

This structure is the single biggest tell. Real people don't write like textbooks.

**How to break it:**

- Start in the middle of the story. Skip the introduction entirely.
- Have an unbalanced structure. Two long sections and one short one. Or just one section with a question at the end.
- Don't summarize. If you said it well the first time, the reader doesn't need a recap.
- End abruptly when the point is made. Not everything needs a bow on top.

### 2.2 The Paragraph Problem

AI writes dense, well-formed paragraphs. 4-6 sentences per paragraph. Proper topic sentences. Logical flow.

Real people, especially on social media and in emails, write in fragments. One thought per line. Short bursts.

**AI style (too dense):**
> Starting a business requires careful planning and execution. Many entrepreneurs underestimate the challenges they'll face in the first year, leading to burnout and financial difficulties. It's important to build a support network early and establish clear financial boundaries between personal and business accounts. Additionally, having a mentor who has been through the process can provide invaluable guidance during the most challenging moments.

**Human style (breathes):**
> I started a business with zero plan.
>
> Made every rookie mistake in the first year. Burned through savings. Mixed personal and business accounts like an idiot.
>
> The one thing that saved me? A mentor who had already screwed up the same way I was about to.

The differences:

- Shorter sentences
- One thought per line
- White space between thoughts
- Self-deprecating honesty
- No "additionally" or "it's important to"
- Specific details instead of generic advice

### 2.3 The List Problem

AI defaults to numbered lists and bullet points for everything. Often with exactly 3 or 5 items (AI loves odd numbers).

Each item is perfectly parallel in structure. Same length. Same grammatical pattern.

**AI list:**
1. **Prioritize clear communication** — Ensure your team understands expectations
2. **Foster a culture of accountability** — Hold regular check-ins to track progress
3. **Invest in professional development** — Provide opportunities for growth
4. **Embrace feedback loops** — Create mechanisms for continuous improvement
5. **Lead by example** — Demonstrate the behaviors you expect from others

**Human list:**
▷ Talk to your team. Actually talk. Not just Slack messages.
▷ If someone's underperforming, tell them. Don't wait for the performance review.
▷ Your team watches what you do, not what you say in all-hands meetings.

The differences:

- Items aren't parallel in structure. That's fine.
- Different lengths. Some are one sentence. Some are two.
- No bold headers inside each item
- Conversational, not instructional
- Three items, not five. Not everything needs to be comprehensive.

### 2.4 The Transition Problem

AI uses smooth transitions between every idea. "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "That said," "On the other hand."

Real writing uses abrupt shifts. A new paragraph. A single word. Or just moves on.

**AI transitions to never use:**
- Furthermore
- Moreover
- Additionally
- That being said
- It's also worth considering
- On the flip side
- In addition to this
- Building on that point
- This brings us to
- With that in mind

**Human transitions:**
- New paragraph (no transition word needed)
- "But." (one word, its own line)
- "Here's the problem."
- "Then it got worse."
- Just start the next thought. The reader can follow.

## PART 3: THE TONE PROBLEM

### 3.1 AI Is Too Balanced

AI hedges everything. It presents "both sides." It qualifies every statement. It never takes a hard stance.

**AI tone:**
> While there are certainly benefits to remote work, it's important to consider the potential drawbacks as well. Many leaders find that a hybrid approach can offer the best of both worlds, though the optimal solution will vary depending on your team's specific needs and circumstances.

**Human tone:**
> Remote work killed our team culture. We tried everything. Hybrid Wednesdays. Virtual coffee chats. Online team-building games nobody wanted to play.
>
> What fixed it? Mandatory in-person weeks once a quarter. Not popular. But it worked.

The difference is conviction. Real people have opinions. They take stances. They don't "on the other hand" everything.

### 3.2 AI Is Too Polished

AI never makes grammar mistakes. Every sentence is syntactically perfect. Every comma is placed correctly. Every thought is fully formed.

Real people:
- Start sentences with "And" or "But"
- Use fragments. Like this.
- Skip apostrophes sometimes (dont instead of don't)
- Leave a slightly rough transition that they didn't bother to smooth out
- Use "like" or "basically" occasionally
- Write "its" when they mean "it's" or vice versa

**Important:** Don't plant fake mistakes. That's worse than being too polished. Just don't over-edit. Write naturally and leave the small imperfections that come with speed.

### 3.3 AI Is Too Positive

AI defaults to encouragement. Everything is "a great opportunity." Every failure "was actually a blessing in disguise." Every challenge is "exciting."

Real writing includes:
- Admitting something sucked
- Calling out what didn't work without a silver lining
- Being frustrated
- Saying "I don't know"
- Being critical without adding "but here's the positive side"

**AI ending:**
> While the journey has been challenging, I'm grateful for every experience that has shaped me into the leader I am today. I'm excited for what the future holds!

**Human ending:**
> Was it worth it? Most days, honestly, I'm not sure. But I'm not going back to corporate. That I know.

### 3.4 AI Avoids Specifics, Humans Live in Them

AI generalizes. "Many leaders struggle with..." "Teams often face challenges when..."

Humans use specific details. "$75k in debt." "3,354 coaching sessions." "A 20-year generation gap." "He co-founded a gaming platform during university."

**Rule:** If you can replace a vague phrase with a specific number, name, timeframe, or detail, do it. Every time.

| AI (vague) | Human (specific) |
|---|---|
| Many years of experience | 20 years in software |
| Significant revenue growth | $537k across 9 streams |
| Various engineering leaders | 295 engineering leaders since 2019 |
| Recently transitioned careers | Left a $350k job offer in 2023 |
| Helped multiple companies | Helped Mews Series C, Manta/IBM acquisition |

## PART 4: THE PUNCTUATION AND FORMATTING TELLS

### 4.1 The Dash Problem

AI loves em dashes. It uses them constantly as separators, parenthetical insertions, and dramatic pauses.

> The challenge — one that many leaders face — is finding the right balance between technical depth and strategic thinking.

Real people use periods. Or commas. Or just start a new line.

> The challenge is real. Finding the right balance between technical depth and strategic thinking. Most leaders I coach struggle with exactly this.

**Rule:** If you see a dash being used as a separator in your text, replace it with a period or a line break. Compound words with hyphens (self-deprecating, co-founder) are fine.

### 4.2 The Bold and Header Problem

AI over-formats. Bold text everywhere. Headers for every section. Structured like a Wikipedia article.

For social media and casual writing:

- Skip the headers unless it's genuinely a long reference document
- Bold only 1-2 key phrases per post, if any
- Let the white space and line breaks do the structural work

### 4.3 The Emoji Problem

AI either avoids emojis entirely or sprinkles them randomly for "energy."

**AI emoji use (bad):**
> 🚀 Excited to share my top 5 leadership tips! 💪🎯
>
> 1️⃣ Communication is key! 🗣️
> 2️⃣ Lead by example! ⭐
> 3️⃣ Embrace change! 🔄

**Human emoji use (good):**
> 👉 Can you pitch yourself in 60 seconds without buzzwords?

One emoji. Functional. Points the reader to the question.

### 4.4 The Quotation Mark Problem

AI wraps concepts in quotes for emphasis when no actual quote exists.

> The idea of "servant leadership" has become a "must-have" for modern organizations looking to "transform" their culture.

Real people quote actual things people said. Or use no quotes at all.

> Servant leadership sounds great in books. On the ground, it means your 1:1 is about their problems, not your status update.

## PART 5: THE OPENING AND CLOSING PROBLEM

### 5.1 AI Openings (Kill These)

AI has a handful of opening templates it rotates through:

- "In today's [adjective] world of [topic]..."
- "As a [role], I've learned that..."
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "[Topic] is one of the most important..."
- "Let me share a story about..."
- "I recently had the opportunity to..."
- "There's a common misconception about..."

**Human openings that work:**

- Start with a specific scene: "A first-time engineering manager asked me..."
- Start with a number: "I was $75k in debt 3 years ago."
- Start with a bold claim: "Your 1:1s are project meetings."
- Start mid-action: "I stopped him mid-pitch."
- Start with a contradiction: "I left a $350k job offer."

The key: be specific from word one. No warm-up. No throat-clearing.

### 5.2 AI Closings (Kill These Too)

AI closes with:
- "In conclusion..."
- "To sum up..."
- "Remember, the key is to..."
- "I'd love to hear your thoughts!"
- "What strategies have worked for you?"
- "Here's to [positive outcome]!"
- "The journey continues..."

**Human closings:**

- A question that actually makes the reader think: "Can you pitch yourself in 60 seconds without buzzwords?"
- A punchy one-liner: "Different wiring. Same leadership potential."
- Self-deprecating humor
- An abrupt stop after the final insight. No wrap-up needed.
- "Was it worth it? Most days I'm not sure."

## PART 6: CONTENT PATTERNS THAT SCREAM AI

### 6.1 The "Three Pillars" Pattern

AI loves grouping things into exactly three categories with alliterative or parallel names.

> The three pillars of effective leadership: Communication, Collaboration, and Commitment.

Real frameworks are messier. They have 2 steps or 7 steps. The names don't all start with the same letter. One step is longer than the others.

### 6.2 The "On One Hand / On the Other Hand" Pattern

AI is allergic to taking sides. It presents everything as a balanced debate.

Real writing picks a side. Even if you acknowledge complexity, you still end with a clear stance.

### 6.3 The "As Someone Who" Pattern

> As someone who has spent over two decades in the tech industry...
> As a leader who values transparency...
> As an engineering coach with hundreds of sessions under my belt...

Real people don't introduce themselves in every paragraph. They just say what they mean and let their experience show through the specificity of their examples.

### 6.4 The "Imagine" Pattern

> Imagine a world where engineering managers actually listened to their teams...
> Picture this: You're in a 1:1, and your direct report finally opens up...

AI loves "imagine" and "picture this" as narrative devices. Humans just describe what happened.

> My mentee walked into the 1:1 looking exhausted. First words out of his mouth: "I think I'm going to quit."

Scene, not imagination.

### 6.5 The Perfect Example Problem

AI examples are too clean. Too illustrative. They hit every point perfectly.

Real examples are messy. They have details that don't perfectly serve the narrative. They include a side note or a human moment that has nothing to do with the "lesson."

> His pitch was solid. Co-founded a gaming platform during university. Led the dev team. Acquired in 18 months. Scaled from 3 to 8 engineers. Inherited 100k daily users, 30 microservices, almost no docs. Made it work.

Notice "almost no docs" — a specific, messy detail that makes it feel real.

## PART 7: THE EDITING CHECKLIST

After writing (or after AI generates a draft), run through this:

**Vocabulary scan:**
- [ ] Search for every word from the banned list. Replace or delete.
- [ ] Check for multi-syllable words that have simple alternatives. Swap them.
- [ ] Look for "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally." Delete or replace with a line break.

**Structure scan:**
- [ ] Does it have an introduction paragraph? Cut it. Start with the hook.
- [ ] Does it end with a summary? Cut it. End with the punchline.
- [ ] Are all list items the same length and structure? Make them uneven.
- [ ] Are there exactly 3 or 5 points? Consider whether a different number is more honest.

**Tone scan:**
- [ ] Does every paragraph feel balanced and fair? Pick a side somewhere.
- [ ] Is the ending positive and hopeful? Consider ending with honesty instead.
- [ ] Are there specific numbers, names, or details? Add more.
- [ ] Would you actually say this sentence out loud? If not, rewrite it.

**Formatting scan:**
- [ ] Any dashes used as separators? Replace with periods or line breaks.
- [ ] Dense paragraphs of 4+ lines? Break them up.
- [ ] Too many bold phrases or headers? Strip most of them.
- [ ] Random emojis that don't serve a function? Remove them.

**The final test:**
Read the whole thing out loud. If you stumble over a phrase, it's too formal. If a sentence feels like it belongs in a textbook, rewrite it. If you can hear the AI, so can your reader.

## PART 8: QUICK REFERENCE CARD

**Always do:**
- Use simple words
- Start with a specific detail or scene
- Take a clear stance
- Include real numbers
- Write one thought per line
- Leave small imperfections alone
- End with a punchline, question, or honest statement

**Never do:**
- Use words from the banned list
- Write a balanced "on the other hand" take
- Open with "In today's..." or "Let me share..."
- Use dashes as separators
- Format like a Wikipedia article
- Summarize at the end what you already said
- Make every example perfectly illustrative
- Use emojis for decoration

**When editing AI output:**
1. Delete the first paragraph (AI always throat-clears)
2. Delete the last paragraph (AI always summarizes)
3. Search and destroy banned words
4. Break dense paragraphs into single lines
5. Add one specific detail or number per section
6. Remove at least half the transition words
7. Read it out loud. Rewrite anything you'd never actually say.
